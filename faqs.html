Frequently asked questions

This section includes common concerns about doing business in East Africa.

General Questions

1.Is InCA an advertising agency?

No we're not. Advertising is about persuading a buyer to choose one brand of a product or service in preference to another. By contrast, we are specialists in communication, whether for commercial clients, Governments or NGOs. This involves the creation of messages in a variety of media to articulate a corporate vision or call to action. On many occasions we are subcontracted by advertising agencies to produce work that fits into a broader campaign, but advertising in not our core business.

2.How safe is it to film in Kenya?

Evers since Kenya's military incursion into southern Somalia, the country has become vulnerable to terrorist attacks, particularly on the coast. As a result, public buildings, hotels and shopping centres are subject to rigorous security checks on all visitors. Before travelling, it is best to check local sources for the current level of risk in the area that you plan to visit.
Like all countries where the distribution of income and power is very unequal, Kenya has always had inherent security risks. Furthermore, there is a natural assumption that if you are from abroad and carrying technical equipment, you are likely to be wealthy. It's therefore wise to take precautions: hiring a local driver who â€˜knows the ropes' and can look after equipment is little short of mandatory. In the evenings, do not leave valuables in your room but put them in a hotel safe if at all possible. When driving, keep your car doors locked and your windows completely closed unless you're driving on the open road. It is very unwise to walk alone after dark, particularly if you are in urban areas.
If you observe these precautions, it is unlikely that you will be at significant risk, unless you are visiting the country specifically to document political unrest.

3.Where outside Kenya does InCA work?

Over the last two decades, we have worked in many African countries, including Uganda, Tanzania, Mozambique, Zimbabwe, South Africa, Ghana, Mali and Senegal. We have many contacts in these countries, and are fully conversant with the procedures involved in securing permission to film.
Our consultancy arm has also worked in several different African countries, as well as the United States.

4.Why is a quality film so expensive?

The price of filming equipment has dropped significantly in recent years, but this has simply enabled a larger number of untrained people to create films that are not of the highest technical quality. A truly professional film with dedicated audio recording, lighting and considered post-production still involves the input of several people with their own skills. They cannot compete on price with 'one person operations', who tend to use semi-professional equipment
